🔬 What is an owl pellet?

Owls swallow prey whole but cannot digest bones, fur, and feathers. They compress this material into a compact pellet and regurgitate it 6–10 hours after eating. Each pellet contains the skeletal remains of 1–3 prey animals.

🌿 Why study them?

Pellets let us reconstruct food webs without harming any animals. By identifying bones, we can determine exactly what the owl ate, calculate energy flow, and monitor local rodent populations as ecosystem health indicators.

🔍 Field Guide — Bone Types

💀
Skull The most diagnostic bone. Shape and teeth reveal the species.
🦷
Jaw (Mandible) Lower jawbone. Tooth shape confirms diet (herbivore vs. insectivore).
🦴
Long Bones Femur (thigh), humerus (upper arm), tibia (shin). Indicate body size.
〰️
Ribs Curved, thin bones. Count helps estimate number of prey.
⬡
Vertebrae Small spool-shaped spinal bones. Many per prey animal.
🔲
Scapula Shoulder blade — flat, triangular. Paired (one per side).
💡 Scientist tip: Finding two skulls means the owl ate two prey animals in one meal!

🗂️ Sorting Tips

💀
Skulls Large rounded bone with eye sockets and teeth. Most distinctive shape.
🦷
Jaws Curved or L-shaped bone with a row of teeth along one edge.
🦴
Long Bones Rod-shaped with expanded rounded ends (epiphyses). Larger than ribs.
⬡
Small Bones Ribs (thin curved arcs), vertebrae (tiny spools), and flat scapulae.
